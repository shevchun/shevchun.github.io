panic hardware goes by many names such as panic bars, exit devices, or push bars but no matter which name you use, the main idea behind panic hardware is to provide a quick and safe means of egress for people attempting to exit an area in an emergency. The origins of panic hardware are directly related to mass casualty tragedies in America and the United Kingdom. The single largest loss of life due to a building fire in the history of the US was the 1903 Iroquois Theater fire in Chicago - and it could’ve been prevented with panic hardware.

Many patrons inside the building died because they were unable to operate the complex Bascule locks on the fire exits or simply became trapped as the crowd surged against the doors. As a result, 602 people lost their lives that day. After this tragic event, many buildings started using panic hardware in their buildings so that occupants can easily operate the emergency exits by simply pushing against the bar on the door. Soon, regulatory agencies required panic bars to be installed across almost all commercial buildings in America.

Now in present-day America, numerous coding regulations dictate how and when panic hardware should be installed. Here are several applications that are commonly regulated by building codes and some examples of situations you may encounter in your commercial building that require panic hardware.

Common Applications for Panic Hardware

Depending on your location, you may have to abide by the International Building Code (IBC), National Fire Protection Association (NFPA), and other Authority Having Jurisdictions (AHJs).

Regulations can change from state to state or even city to city, so be sure to check with your local regulatory agency to determine exactly what codes you need to adhere to when installing panic hardware in your commercial business.

For jurisdictions following the 2006 and above IBC, you are required to install panic hardware in the following situations and occupancy classifications:

  • Assembly applications where there are 50 or more occupants
  • Educational applications where there are 50 or more occupants
  • High-hazard areas where any number of occupants may need quick egress

For jurisdictions following the NFPA 101 Life Safety Code, you are required to install panic hardware in the following situations and occupancy types:

  1. Assembly applications where there are 50 or more occupants
  2. Educational applications where there are 50 or more occupants
  3. High-hazard areas where any number of occupants may need quick egress

With that being said, you may commonly see push bars installed in applications where the IBC or other regulatory agencies have not designated a requirement.

Assembly Applications

The IBC requires panic hardware to be installed in applications where there is an assembly occupancy of 50 or more. Assembly here refers to rooms or buildings that have large gatherings of people such as restaurants, concert halls, places of worship, healthcare facilities, retail spaces, government buildings, and more.

Depending on the purpose of the door in your assembly, you may prefer to use one type of panic bar over another. It’s common to find models such as the Sargent 8815 installed on doors used to exit a large occupancy room.

Commercial businesses almost always want to know who is entering and exiting a building and alarmed exit devices prevent customers from leaving through a rear exit without an employee’s knowledge.

For emergency situations where the door leads to the exterior of the building, an alarmed exit device like the Detex V40 is often preferable.

Educational Applications

An example of double doors using a surface-mounted vertical rod exit device without a mullion. [Source]

Large educational institutions such as daycares, private schools, and public schools are required to use panic hardware in large rooms with educational occupancies of 50 or more. These rooms are often cafeterias, gymnasiums, and large lecture halls.

Emergency exits in educational institutions also require panic devices just like emergency exits in other commercial buildings. Depending on your needs, you may opt for a regular push bar exit device or an alarmed exit device.

If you do decide to choose an alarmed exit device, a delayed egress alarm exit device such as the Alarm Lock 715 may be better. Delayed egress devices will allow students and faculty to exit in emergencies, but will prevent students from leaving without alerting staff.

High-Hazard Areas

Commercial manufacturing buildings often have areas with hazardous materials or dangerously high amounts of electricity. In these situations, the NFPA does not specify a minimum amount of high-hazard occupancies. Even a small room that is used as a battery room is required to have panic bars installed so that any workers inside the room can quickly leave in the direction of egress to a safe area.

The NFPA 70 National Electric Code has been adopted by all 50 states and dictates that panic hardware needs to be installed no matter the occupant load on doors within twenty-five feet of work areas housing:

  • Electrical equipment using 600 or more volts
  • Electrical equipment with less than 600 volts but more than 800 amperes
  • Battery rooms

Rim panic bars or rim exit devices, such as the Sargent 8813, are commonly used for these applications since the exterior of the door can be locked with keys restricting access to these sensitive electrical rooms, but the interior of the door can always be opened with a push bar providing the occupants' free egress.

 

Fire Exit Hardware

An example of alarmed fire exit hardware installed on doors in a commercial building.[Source]

Panic hardware installed on fire exits is referred to as fire exit hardware since they are compliant with UL 305 and UL 10C. These standards refer to ratings given to panic hardware after it has been tested by a certified testing facility. 

UL 305 is a standard that all panic hardware must pass and signifies that the device has passed an endurance test, emergency operation test, and temperature test. UL 10C denotes that it can retard the passage of fire and is safe to install on a fire-rated door.

 

Delayed Egress Panic Hardware

The IBC and NFPA typically prohibit delayed egress panic hardware in assembly, educational, or high-hazard use groups - but there are exceptions. Buildings with automatic fire suppression systems are allowed to install approved delayed egress systems on fire door assemblies.

Models like the Alarm Lock 715-US28 are used on emergency exits in large stores where theft or unauthorized exit is a real concern and delaying a thief’s escape is needed.

This model will alert employees or security that someone is attempting to exit the store, but it will still allow building occupants to exit in life-threatening situations.

Other Building Code Considerations

The IBC and the NFPA 101 Life Safety Code also maintain the following code requirements for panic hardware or fire exit hardware installed in commercial constructions:

  • Installed panic hardware needs to be in accordance with UL 305
  • Installed fire exit devices need to be in accordance with UL 10C and UL 305
  • It shall take no more than 15lbs of force to operate the touchpad/crossbar/pushpad of the panic device
  • The actuating portion of the panic device shall be at least half of the width of the door leaf or more
  • No additional locking device may be installed that will prevent the door from opening when the push bar, touchpad, or crossbar of the panic device is pressed except in the following instances:
    • Controlled egress exit devices installed in certain types of healthcare buildings
    • Delayed egress locks commonly found in commercial stores and other applications as discussed above
    • Electromagnetic locking mechanism activated by a sensor in the hardware of the panic device

Final Thoughts
